    The filling order

    Orbitals are seats; three rules fill them. The seats shift with the nucleus, and that shift makes the transition row.

    Pauli Hund aufbau shielding

    Chapter 1 built the seats; now fill them. Watch energies shift with $Z$ and see why 4s fills before 3d in potassium yet leaves first in iron.

    2.1 Two electrons per orbital

    Electrons carry spin, $\uparrow$ or $\downarrow$ (not literal rotation). Pauli: no two share a quantum state. An orbital fixes $(n, \ell, m)$; it holds two only with opposite spin:

    ↑ ↓ one orbital, two opposite spins

    Counts: s=2, p=6, d=10, f=14 — the block widths of the periodic table.

    2.2 Shielding breaks the tie

    In hydrogen, energy depends only on $n$: 2s and 2p are degenerate. Add electrons and that breaks. Inner electrons screen the nucleus; the outer one feels an effective charge:

    $$Z_\mathrm{eff} = Z - \htmlData{tip=The shielding constant - how much pull the inner electrons cancel}{S}$$

    Penetration decides the rest: s dips deepest and feels nearly full nucleus; p less; d barely. $Z_\mathrm{eff}$ differs by subshell:

    Within one shell

    $E_{ns} < E_{np} < E_{nd} < E_{nf}$. More penetration, lower energy.

    Diagram

    Why s sees more nucleus than d

    s pokes inside the core and feels bare nucleus; d stays outside, screened.

    2.3 The seating rules

    Three rules decide the ground state:

    • Aufbau: each electron takes the lowest open seat.
    • Pauli: two per orbital, opposite spins.
    • Hund: across equal orbitals, spread before pairing — pairing costs energy.

    2.4 The rungs move

    The ladder is not fixed. Subshell energies shift with $Z$ at different rates: 4s gently, 3d fast once it fills. The curves cross between K and Sc.

    About the model

    Energies use Slater's rules (1930), an empirical $Z_\mathrm{eff}$ approximation. Real atoms need Hartree–Fock; Slater catches the crossings on one page.

    Key insight

    Aufbau is not a list. Each electron takes the lowest seat when it arrives. At $Z=19$ that's 4s; by $Z=21$ it's 3d.

    2.5 Last in, first out — except

    Fe → Fe2+ loses both 4s electrons before touching 3d — yet 4s filled first. Why leave first too?

    By iron ($Z=26$), 3d has plunged below 4s. The 4s pair are now the highest electrons, and ionization takes the highest. Cheap to fill, expensive to keep.

    2.6 The exceptions prove the rule

    Two transition-row elements break the chart:

    • Chromium ($Z=24$): not [Ar] 3d4 4s2, but [Ar] 3d5 4s1.
    • Copper ($Z=29$): not [Ar] 3d9 4s2, but [Ar] 3d10 4s1.

    Near $Z=24$ and $Z=29$, 4s and 3d sit very close. A half-filled or full d lowers repulsion (electrons in different orbitals avoid each other). Promoting one 4s to 3d gives Cr a half d, Cu a full d. Repulsion saved beats the climb.

    The real rule

    Atoms minimize total energy. Aufbau holds when gaps are large; when gaps shrink, repulsion gets a vote. The law is energy minimization, not the chart.

    2.7 What you now have

    • Pauli: two electrons per orbital, opposite spins.
    • Shielding + penetration: $E_{ns} < E_{np} < E_{nd}$ within a shell.
    • Aufbau + Hund + Pauli give ground-state configurations.
    • 4s/3d crossing: 4s fills first, ionizes first.
    • Cr and Cu deviate: total energy wins.
