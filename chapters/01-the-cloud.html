    1.1 The atom is mostly nothing

    An atom is $10^{-10}$ m across; its nucleus, $10^{-15}$ m, holds nearly all the mass. Marble at center field, electrons fill the stadium.

    The real question: what are the electrons doing in all that space? The periodic table and bonding both answer it.

    1.2 The obvious guess destroys itself

    Opposite charges attract by an inverse-square force, like gravity. Obvious model: a solar system, electrons orbiting the nucleus.

    Classical physics kills it. An orbiting electron constantly accelerates, and accelerating charges radiate energy (a radio antenna works this way). It broadcasts energy and spirals in.

    Sixteen picoseconds. Matter would not exist. Second failure: orbits allow any radius, yet every hydrogen atom is identical. Orbits can't pick specific states; waves can.

    1.3 Waves that close on themselves

    A guitar string vibrates in fixed patterns: one bump, two, three. Half a bump is impossible — the ends are pinned. That shape is a standing wave; pinning the ends quantizes it into whole-number bumps.

    In 1924 de Broglie proposed electrons are waves too; experiments confirmed it within three years. Run the orbit idea for a wave. A closed loop has no pinned ends, but a harsher rule: after one lap the wave meets itself and had better line up.

    $$\htmlData{tip=The distance around the loop - its circumference}{2\pi r} = n\,\htmlData{tip=The electron's wavelength}{\lambda}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

    Whole number of wavelengths: the wave reinforces. Anything else: it cancels itself.

    Key insight

    Quantization is not bolted on. Confined waves do this: only specific patterns survive, so the electron has only specific energies. That is why every hydrogen atom matches.

    1.4 Orbitals: standing waves in 3D

    The loop is a cartoon. A real wave fills 3D space, pulled inward by the nucleus. Schrödinger (1926) gives the true patterns: orbitals.

    Definition

    An orbital is a 3D standing-wave pattern an electron can occupy. The wave $\htmlData{tip=The Greek letter psi - the wave's value at each point in space}{\psi}$ has a value everywhere; $|\psi|^2$ is the probability of finding the electron there. Not a path — the electron's "location" is the cloud.

    Three whole-number labels:

    • $n$ — size and energy (1, 2, 3, ...). Bigger $n$, bigger cloud, higher energy.
    • $\ell$ — shape family (0 to $n-1$): ℓ = 0 → s ℓ = 1 → p ℓ = 2 → d ℓ = 3 → f. These colors follow each family throughout.
    • $m$ — orientation ($-\ell$ to $+\ell$). One s, three p, five d, seven f.

    Two things to notice. Clouds have nodes: surfaces where the wave is zero, like still points on a string. The wave also has a sign: pale regions are negative. $|\psi|^2$ erases sign for one electron, but in chapter 4 it decides whether two atoms' waves reinforce or cancel.

    Looking ahead

    Orbitals sharing $n$ and $\ell$ form a subshell (2p = the three $n=2$, $\ell=1$ orbitals); same-$n$ subshells form a shell. Chapter 2 fills them.

    1.5 How far out is the electron?

    Clouds show shape; one chart shows distance. Plot probability vs radius — patterns appear that drive the next two chapters.

    Interactive

    Radial probability

    Each curve: probability at distance r. Switch comparisons.

    1. Bigger $n$ lives farther out.
    2. Higher-$n$ orbitals keep inner lobes: small humps near the nucleus, one per node. A 3s electron mostly lives far out but part of its cloud dips deep inside.
    3. 4s sits farther out than 3d, yet its inner lobes reach closer to the nucleus than 3d ever does. That sneaking-in is penetration.
    Key insight

    Penetration is not a footnote. A penetrating orbital feels the nucleus's full pull where others feel a screened version. That builds the periodic table.

    1.6 What you now have

    • Orbits radiate and collapse; quantized states are required.
    • Confined waves quantize themselves.
    • An orbital is a 3D standing wave (n, ℓ, m); probability is $|\psi|^2$.
    • Nodes, inner lobes, penetration — fuel for chapter 2.
